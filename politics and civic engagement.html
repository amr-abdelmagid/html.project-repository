Some scientists and activists argue that the Internet of Things can be used to create new paradigms of civic engagement if device networks can be open to user control and interoperable platforms. Philip N. wrote. Howard, professor and author, states that political life in both democracies and authoritarian regimes will be shaped by the way the Internet of Things is used for civic participation. For that to happen, he argues that any connected device must be able to disclose the "end beneficiaries" list of its sensor data and that individual citizens must be able to add new organizations to the beneficiary list. In addition, he argues that civil society groups need to start developing their Internet of Things strategy in order to take advantage of data and interact with the public
